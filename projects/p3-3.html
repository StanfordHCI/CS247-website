Part III is an exercise in rapid prototyping — how do you convey the core of your idea without sinking in untold hours?

Three prototypes

Much like with Horcruxes, one should not place all their faith in a single prototype. As we've discussed in class, having multiple parallel prototypes produces better final designs.

Your goal now is to prototype all three of your design ideas. The explicit goal of this assignment is to challenge ourselves to generate convincing but rapid prototypes. Your goal is not to create a mockup or scale model, but to create experiences to which your users can react.

The crux of this assignment will be figuring out how to prototype each idea quickly and convincingly, without sinking untold hours into them. As in class, before you start the prototype, identify: what's your design ideas's biggest unanswered question? Use that quesiton to figure out what kind of prototype you would need to build and how you would get feedback from users to answer it. Based on the question, decide upon the best method of prototyping the idea. By now, you have a wide range of prototyping approaches in your arsenal. Prototypes can be hand-drawn, constructed physically, behavioral, or digital. Your skillset also includes Wizard of Oz prototypes, paper prototypes, video prototypes, and mocks. Which approach or toolkit will best represent each idea? The choice is yours here.

Capture your questions and prototypes so that we can understand what you did. Include relevant materials (e.g., images, videos) and a short description.

Rapid feedback

Remember that testing many can produce better insights than testing one. So, bring your three prototypes to at least four representative users. Ask your studio staff if you need help gaining access to users.

Set the stage — the user's problem or goal — and remind the user that you are trying to test the prototypes, not the users. Take notes as they use each of your prototypes. When they are done, talk with them more holistically about the three prototypes. Find out what resonanted with each prototype relative to the others, and what missed the mark. While you will gather usability feedback, your primary goal is instead to gain insight into the broader design space and your general design ideas.

Write up a summary of your findings. First, describe your method and who you tested with. Second, tell us what you saw. Third, tell us your interpretation and reflections. What did the feedback imply about your original design questions that led to the prototypes? What changes will it prompt? Focus on concept, not usability.

Team dynamics check-in

When you submit this assignment, everyone on the team should take a moment to privately evaluate how the team is doing. Is everyone pulling their weight? Are the collaborations effective? Are communication channels open?

Everyone must fill out our Team Dynamics Check-in along with their milestone. If things could be going better, this gives us as staff a chance to step in and help.

Submit

Make sure you've filled out the team dynamics check-in. Then, pull everything together into your submission folder labeled "P3-3". This includes:

  • A subfolder for each prototype: the question each was trying to ask, and a description (including images) of each prototype.
  • A PDF with your user feedback.

Bring full-page color printouts of your prototypes, and be prepared to discuss the feedback.

Grading rubric

Each milestone is worth five points. You will receive five points for completing the assignment satisfactorily, submitting it, and your whole team bringing your sketches to class for feedback.
